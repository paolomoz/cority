As the sustainability regulatory landscape shifts towards greater accountability and transparency, companies of all sizes are increasingly recognizing the importance of integrating ESG factors into their operations. ESG regulations have increased by 155% in the past decade, with 1,255 ESG regulations introduced worldwide since 2011.

Not just as a matter of compliance, but as a strategic lever driving long-term success and resilience. To take action, companies need to rely on various tools, one of which is benchmarking. By comparing their sustainability performance against industry standards and best practices, organizations gain valuable insights into their sustainability initiatives, making it a driver for transformation.

Benchmarking serves multiple purposes: it helps measuring performance, identifying gaps and best-practices setting realistic goals, and ultimately, driving continuous improvement. However, the journey to effective sustainability benchmarking is paved with challenges. Particularly for smaller companies that may lack the resources and expertise of their larger counterparts.

Why Is Sustainability Benchmarking Important?

Measure Performance Effectively

Benchmarking allows organizations to measure their sustainability performance against industry peers, as well as identify their strengths and weaknesses.

Set Realistic and Achievable Goals

By assessing their current sustainability performance, companies can establish a clear baseline for their goals. This baseline provides a reference point from which to set meaningful and realistic goals, aligned with industry standards and expectations.

The results of benchmarking performance assessments help companies prioritize resource allocation efficiently, focusing on high-impact areas that drive significant sustainability improvements.

Enhance Transparency and Accountability

Benchmarking promotes transparency by providing stakeholders with clear metrics on companies’ sustainability performance, thereby increasing their accountability.

It helps investors assess how a company measures up against sustainability criteria, which can influence their confidence and investment decisions. Especially through due diligence processes. It can also help customers who are becoming more conscious of the sustainability practices of the brands they support. Benchmarking can be a factor in their decision-making process.

Drive Innovation and Competitive Advantage

Having a close look at peers’ practices is an efficient way to identify business opportunities. As a result, it fosters innovation through the adoption of new technologies and practices identified among peers’.

It can also be leveraged as a market differentiator and turned into a competitive advantage. When used to highlight operational inefficiencies, it leads to cost savings ultimately enhancing financial performance. An example would be if a company has significantly higher energy consumption than industry peers.

Challenges in Sustainability Benchmarking

1. Data Availability and Quality

One of the primary challenges in benchmarking is the lack of consistent, high-quality, and publicly available sustainability data. As specified in the IBM Sustainability report published in 2024, “only about four in 10 organizations can automatically source sustainability data”. Private companies often have less rigorous reporting practices compared to public companies, making it difficult to gather reliable data. Recent regulations such as the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) should allow medium-sized companies to overcome this issue. Yet, this challenge is particularly prominent for private equity firms and small companies.

2. Lack of Standardization in Frameworks

The sustainability landscape is characterized by a plethora of standards and frameworks, leading to inconsistencies in reporting and benchmarking practices due to different calculation methodologies for key data points like carbon emissions and employee turnover. This lack of standardization makes it almost impossible to compare companies on a like-for-like basis. Regulations such as CSRD or SFDR have paved the way for greater harmonization of reporting practices. Nonetheless, methodological choices often remain in the hands of individual companies.

3. Resource-Intensive Processes

Conducting comprehensive sustainability benchmarking requires significant human and financial resources. Expertise, time, and technology are just some of the resources a company should invest in.

4. Evolving Regulatory Landscape

Sustainability regulations and factors are continuously evolving. This makes it difficult for benchmarks to stay up to date.

Conclusion

Benchmarking not only aids in performance measurement and goal setting but also drives transparency, accountability, and value creation. Challenges such as data availability and quality, standardization, and resource needs must be addressed by companies to be in a position to take advantage of the benefits it brings.
